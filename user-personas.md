## Primary Persona

<details>
<summary><strong>👩‍💻 Priya, 22 — The Anxious Applicant</strong></summary>

<br>

### Who She Is

Priya is a final-year Computer Science student at a Tier-2 engineering college in Pune, India. She's smart, hardworking, and has done one internship — but she's surrounded by peers who seem more confident, and she genuinely doesn't know if her resume is good enough to get her foot in the door at a tech company.

She's applying for SWE and product roles at companies like Flipkart, Razorpay, and mid-size startups. She's sent out 40+ applications and heard back from 3.

---

### Profile

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Age** | 22 |
| **Location** | Pune, India |
| **Education** | B.Tech Computer Science, Tier-2 college |
| **Experience** | 1 internship (3 months, full-stack dev) |
| **Applying for** | SWE roles, some APM (Associate PM) roles |
| **Income / Budget** | ₹0 disposable income as a student; max ₹300–500/month for tools if she sees value |
| **Devices** | Android phone (primary), Windows laptop (for work) |
| **Tech comfort** | High — uses LinkedIn, GitHub, VS Code, has tried ChatGPT for resume help |

---

### Goals

- Get at least **2–3 interview calls** from her next batch of applications
- Understand **why her resume isn't converting** into callbacks
- Practice interviews so she doesn't freeze on the day
- Do all of this **without spending money she doesn't have**

---

### Frustrations

> *"I've applied to 40 companies. I got 3 responses, and 2 were rejections after the first round. I don't know what I'm doing wrong."*

> *"I used ChatGPT to rewrite my resume but I don't know if it actually helped. It just sounds fancier — I'm not sure if it's matching what companies actually want."*

> *"Every interview prep tool I find costs $25/month. I'm a student. I can't afford that."*

---

### Behaviours

- Scrolls LinkedIn job postings for 30–60 minutes a day
- Applies in batches — sends 10–15 applications on weekends
- Active on Reddit communities like r/cscareerquestions and r/developersIndia
- Has bookmarked several interview prep YouTube channels but hasn't been consistent
- Will try a new tool if a classmate recommends it; highly influenced by peer word-of-mouth

---

### What Success Looks Like

Priya uses the tool on a Sunday evening. She uploads her resume, pastes a Razorpay SWE job description, and sees her match score is 38/100. She spends 45 minutes accepting bullet rewrites and fixing her skills section. Her score goes to 71/100. She runs one mock interview for SWE behavioural round, gets feedback that she's not using the STAR framework consistently.

The following Thursday, she gets an interview call from Razorpay.

**That is the product working.**

---

### Design Implications

- The free tier must deliver the "aha moment" — she cannot be paywalled before she sees value
- Onboarding must be under 3 minutes — she'll bounce if it's complicated
- Mobile experience must be solid — she'll likely first open the tool on her phone
- Language must be encouraging, not clinical — she's already anxious; feedback tone matters

</details>

## Secondary Persona

<details>
<summary><strong>👨‍💼 Marcus, 26 — The Career Switcher</strong></summary>

<br>

### Who He Is

Marcus has spent two years in a B2B marketing role at a mid-size SaaS company in Bangalore. He's good at his job but has always been drawn to the product side — he's the one who ends up in product strategy meetings and has strong opinions on user flows. He's decided to make the pivot to Product Management.

His problem: his resume reads like a marketer's, and he doesn't know how to reframe his experience to look credible to a PM hiring panel.

---

### Profile

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Age** | 26 |
| **Location** | Bangalore, India |
| **Education** | BBA, top-tier college |
| **Experience** | 2 years in B2B SaaS marketing |
| **Applying for** | Associate PM / PM roles at product-led SaaS companies |
| **Income / Budget** | ₹45,000/month salary; willing to spend ₹500–1,000/month on career tools |
| **Devices** | MacBook (primary), iPhone |
| **Tech comfort** | High — uses Notion, Figma (basic), Amplitude, Slack |

---

### Goals

- **Reframe** his marketing experience through a product lens (metrics ownership, user research, GTM strategy)
- Understand what **PM interviewers are actually looking for** — product sense, execution, estimation
- Practice PM interview formats he's never done before (product critique, root cause analysis, estimation)

---

### Frustrations

> *"My resume lists everything I've done in marketing. But when I put it next to a PM job description, it looks completely irrelevant. I know I have the skills — I just don't know how to show them."*

> *"PM interviews are completely different from anything I've done. I don't even know what a good answer to 'design a product for blind users' looks like."*

---

### Behaviours

- Reads PM-focused content on Lenny's Newsletter, Product School blog, and Mind the Product
- Has done 2 mock interviews with friends — got vague feedback ("seemed good")
- More willing to pay for tools than Priya, but still needs to see clear value before subscribing

---

### What Success Looks Like

Marcus pastes a Swiggy PM job description. The AI tells him that 3 of his bullet points can be reframed — "Led GTM strategy for 3 product launches" instead of "Managed marketing campaigns." He practices a product sense interview, gets specific feedback that his answers are too marketing-focused and need more user empathy framing.

Two weeks later he walks into a PM interview at a Series B startup feeling prepared for question types he'd never faced before.

---

### Design Implications

- JD alignment and bullet rewriting are the highest-value features for Marcus
- PM question bank must be high quality — he'll notice if questions feel generic
- His willingness to pay is higher; the Pro conversion pitch should lean into unlimited question bank access and voice mode

</details>

## Anti-Persona

<details>
<summary><strong>🚫 Rohan, 38 — The Senior Executive (Not Our User)</strong></summary>

<br>

### Why We Document This

Anti-personas are as important as primary personas. Knowing who we are **not** building for prevents scope creep, feature bloat, and trying to serve a market that has different needs and different willingness-to-pay.

---

### Who He Is

Rohan is a 12-year engineering veteran who's been a Senior Engineering Manager at a large tech company. He's looking for Director-level or VP-level roles. His resume is 3 pages, covers 4 companies, and requires careful positioning and narrative crafting — not bullet point rewrites.

---

### Why He's Not Our User

| His Need | Why Our Tool Doesn't Serve It |
|----------|------------------------------|
| Executive narrative & personal brand crafting | Our AI is optimised for JD alignment and impact metrics — not C-suite positioning |
| Relationship-led job search (referrals, headhunters) | He doesn't apply through job boards the way students do |
| Compensation negotiation strategy | Out of scope for v1 (and requires human nuance) |
| Interview prep for panel / case-study rounds | Our question bank is entry-to-mid level; executive interview formats are different |
| Willingness to pay $50–$200/month for premium coaching | Our ₹299 price signal actually puts him off — he equates low price with low quality |

---

### What This Means for the Product

- We do not build features to serve Rohan in v1 or v2
- If Rohan signs up and uses the tool, that's fine — but we do not design for him
- Marketing, copywriting, and onboarding should speak to Priya — not to Rohan
- The pricing page should not try to have a "Executive" tier to capture him

</details>

## Shared Insights Across Personas

<details>
<summary><strong>🔑 What all our real users have in common</strong></summary>

<br>

Despite being different people, Priya and Marcus share a set of core truths that should inform every product decision:

1. **They are already anxious.** The job search is stressful. Our tone, copy, and feedback must be encouraging and constructive — never harsh, condescending, or score-obsessed without context.

2. **They have been burned by generic advice before.** Both have Googled "how to write a good resume" and read articles that told them to "use strong action verbs." They know it exists. They need something specific to their actual resume and their actual target role.

3. **They will judge us in the first 5 minutes.** If the first JD scan and bullet rewrite don't clearly show them something they couldn't have figured out themselves, they leave and never come back.

4. **They share tools with their network.** If the product genuinely helps, Priya tells 5 friends in her college WhatsApp group. Marcus posts in a PM community Discord. Word-of-mouth is our primary acquisition channel — which means the product experience itself is the marketing.

5. **They don't want to feel stupid.** Feedback that says "your resume is bad" is useless and alienating. Feedback that says "this bullet is missing a metric — here are 3 ways to add one" is actionable and empowering.

</details>
